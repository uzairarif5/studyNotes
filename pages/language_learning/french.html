Lesson 1

  • 'le' occurs in front of some French singular nouns that begin with a consonant.
  • 'la' occurs in front of all other French singular nouns that begin with a consonant.
  • This permits classification of all French nouns into two classes, called genders, which play a major role in French grammar. Although most nouns do not refer to sex at all, the two noun classes are traditionally labeled masculine for 'le' and feminine for 'la'.
  • l' occurs in front of French singular nouns that begin with a vowel and is pronounced as part of the first syllable of those nouns.
  • This does not mean that a noun that begins with a vowel is neither masculine nor feminine, but simply that there is no distinctive mark of gender present in the definite article preceding it.
  • 'les' occurs in front of French plural nouns.
FrenchEnglish
le restaurantthe restaurant
la ruethe street
l'enfantthe child
les amisthe friends
  • A French verb is usually preceded by a noun or by one of a set of short, unstressed forms which we will define as subject pronouns. These pronouns often refer back to some person or thing previously mentioned or indicated. The subject pronouns must be followed by or preceded by a verb. This provides a handy way, incidentally, of identifying verbs.

  • There are several ways of formulating questions in French. One way is to reverse the positions of the subject pronoun and the verb. This process is called inversion.
Vous avez des nouvelles. Avez-vous des nouvelles?
Il està Lyon maintenant. Est-il à Lyon maintenant?
Nous allons au café. Allons-nous au café?
  • Note that when the 3rd person singular ends with a vowel, a "t" is added between the verb and the pronoun.
Il va bien.Va-t-il bien?
  • Question inversion is possible only with subject pronoun and verb. When the subject is a noun, the noun remains before the verb and the appropriate pronoun must be introduced after the verb to form a question.
FrenchEnglish
Vos parents sont-ils toujours en vacances?Are your parents still on vacation?
Le restaurant est-il bon?Is the restaurant good?
  • The most frequently occurring verb in French is être, to be. Here are its forms, which vary grammatically according to the noun or subject pronoun with which it is tied:

il est, elle est, on est he is, she is, one is
nous sommes we are
vous êtes you are
ils sont, elles sont they are

    • Number:
      • Plural of most French adjectives is formed by adding an -s to the singular form.
      • Adjectives ending in s or x in the singular do not change in the plural.
      • Adjectives ending in eau in the masculine singular add an x to form the masculine plural.

Lesson 8

  • Adjective Positions: In French, some adjectives:
    • Follow the noun (post-nominal) (une valise verte).
    • Precede the noun (pre-nominal) (premier étage).
    • Some adjectives can occasionally change their position for stylistic reasons (des vacances excellentes, d'excellentes vacances).
    • Some adjectives are used in different positions according to the nouns they qualify (la prochaine fois, la semaine prochaine).
    • Have different meanings according to their position:
        AdjectivesPre-nominalPost-nominal
        seul only one alone
        cher dear expensive
        ancien former old, ancient
        vrai real true
        dernier last of a series last, preceding
  • The object pronoun en is used instead of 'le, la, les' if the noun it replaces is used in an indefinite or partitive sense:
      Voilà des billets. Here are some tickets.
      En voilà. Here are some.
      Voilà les billets. Here are the tickets.
      Les voilà. Here they are.
      Il achète du pain. He is buying some bread.
      Il en achète. He is buying some.
      Il achète le pain. He is buying the bread.
      Il l' achète He is buying it.
  • The object pronoun en is also used when the noun is preceded by a number or an adverb of quantity:
      Donnez-moi quatre chemises. Give me four shirts.
      Donnez-m'en quatre. Give me four.
      Nous avons plusieurs chambres libres. We have several rooms vacant.
      Nous en avons plusieurs de libres. We have several vacant.
  • en can be used for 'de + location', meaning from there:
      Il vient du Midi. He is coming from the south of France.
      Il en vient. He is coming from there.
      Il part du bureau à 6 heures. He leaves the office at 6 o'clock.
      Il en part à 6 heures. He leaves it at 6 o'clock.
  • en is used with verbs followed by 'de', even if the noun or pronoun is used in a definite way:
      Il a besoin de l'auto. He needs the car.
      Il en a besoin. He needs it.
      Il a besoin de celles-ci. He needs these.
      Il en a besoin. He needs them.
      Elle parle du discours. She's talking about the speech.
      Elle en parle. She's talking about it.
  • Verb forms in ez occurring initially and neither preceded nor followed by the subject pronoun 'vous' are used to indicate a request or a command.
  • Similarly, ons forms in the same frame are regarded as imperatives, with the meaning 'Let ' s ...'
  • Object pronouns follow affirmative imperatives and precede negative imperatives:
      Parlez-lui ce soir. Speak to him tonight.
      Ne lui parlez pas ce soir. Don't speak to him tonight.
      Donnons-lui le numéro. Let's give him the number.
      Ne lui donnons pas le numéro. Let's not give him the number.
